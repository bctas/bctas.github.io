Budgerigar Tips

 

 Welcome to our 'Budgerigar Tips Page' in which we will give you tips to help both you and your Budgerigars.

F1 Stud Budgerigar Web Page Tips (Issues 2 to 7):

ISSUE 7 TIPS - Aviary Management

[1] - Naphthalene Flakes placed in tins with holes punched in them are a deterrent to mice and moths in aviaries and breeding rooms that are not mouse proof.   Remember to shake the tin daily.

[2] - Metal aviaries should be hosed down on days of high temperature -  this will ensure birds do not get overheated and stressed.

[3] - When designing a new aviary try to ensure it is constructed to allow a flow of ventilation through the whole complex.  This will keep the aviary cool and reduce dust.

[4] - It is well worth it to put a 'Spinaway Ventilator' in the roof of the breeding room - as it  helps in ensuring good ventilation and assists in removing dust, etc.

[5] - Before and after sweeping out breeding rooms always sprinkle a bit of water around on the floor - it helps to cut down all dust.

ISSUE 6 TIPS - Supplementary Feeding

[1] - As we all know budgerigars in the wild feed on seeding grasses.   Try cutting wild grasses which are going to seed and feed them to your birds, they love them!

[2] - If you have surplus carrots in the garden or they are going to seed throw bunches of them into your aviary (with the dirt still on them), but make sure you don't do this around the show season.   They will also demolish red beet, sweet corn, and cabbages.

[3] - Try putting gum tree branches in your aviary they will not only smell good, but the birds will have great fun chewing all the leaves and bark off, some also say the eucalyptus oil is a good for the birds.

[4] - We hear there is a new variety of oats available which by all reports is of good quality.   They are called Bandicoot Oats.

[5] - Some parent birds will kick their young out of the nest well before they can crack seed for themselves , and if the cock bird does not feed these young you can crush sweet plain biscuits with a rolling pin.  Mix with water until a thick paste, and then feed this to these young via a crop needle three to four times a day until you see them cracking seed.

ISSUE 5 TIPS - Breeding

[1] - Budgerigars lay every second day, usually between 12 noon and 2 pm.

[2] - To check fertility of eggs use a fine tipped laser torch or similar, eggs will start to show colour on the sixth day of sitting.  If an egg is fertile you will start to see red inside the egg (this is the baby bird forming), if the egg is empty the eggs colour will be clear.

[3] - Hens may not start to sit until the second or third egg is laid, so first egg may take twenty days to hatch.

[4] - We sometimes remove the first and second egg as laid and replace them when third egg is laid, this results in multiple hatchings on day 1.

[5] - If found within a couple of days after laying chipped eggs can be repaired successfully by patching with clear nail polish, ensure polish is dry before placing egg back under hen.

ISSUE 4 TIPS - Show Preparation

[1] - Start whilst birds are still in nest = give those with potential extra handling.

[2] - Introduce these birds to show cages immediately they leave the nest.

[3] - When birds are being show cage trained put them in an area of high human activity, this tends to help them settle down a lot quicker.

[4] - Check birds eight weeks prior to shows for damaged feathers, remove all broken or damaged feathers, remember it takes six to eight weeks for primary flight & tail feathers to regrow to maturity.

[5] - Start a program of spraying the birds with tempered water immediately, one to two days a week building up to four to five days a week one week prior to show date.  Cease spraying two days prior to show.

[6] - As mentioned in Issue Two tips de-spot the birds of surplus throat spots.   I prefer cutting them as this enables the bird to be shown at many shows with only minor touch ups required in this area, depending how multi spotted the birds are dictates when the de-spotting should be undertaken.  It can take two to five goes to get it right, we like to do it slowly placing the birds back in show cages after de-spotting to let the bird settle down so that we can check that we are removing the right spots.

[7] - Having done all this the rest is up to the birds. "good luck"

ISSUE 3 TIPS

[1] - Feather Pluckers - When first noticed place an iodine bell, minus the wire, in the concave which the chicks, this will on most occasions stop the problem.
 
[2] - Budgerigars love bread of all types, try throwing your bread scraps onto the aviary floor, the birds love them.
 
[3] - Having trouble with mite in the breeding room?  Hang a couple of pest strips in the room and this will ensure a mite free breeding season.
 
[4] - Egg Bound Hens - When first noticed immediately immerse the bird, up to the waist, into warm water, this tends to relax the bird and she will usually pass the egg virtually straight away, she must then be rested for at least two months.
 
[5] - Before pairing up your birds, try placing a couple of rotten logs of wood in with the hens, they take great delight in demolishing them and really gets them in the mood to go to nest.
 

ISSUE 2 TIPS

[1] - Try feeding a chicken carcass to your birds in the flight [they will love them].

[2] - For ease of operation during the breeding season attach a small piece of white board contact to each nest box and use a special white board pencil to record daily occurrences.   You can see a picture of a typical budgerigar breeding cage on the BCT Library.

[3] - Instead of plucking out surplus spots when dressing birds for shows, try cutting them with small scissors. By doing it this way, you will only need to dress the birds once per show season, as the bulk of the feather stays intact until a natural moult.

[4] - Having trouble with hens eating their eggs or the cocks throwing eggs out? Then try replacing the eggs with marbles, ' They will soon give up!'

[5] - If you have tight sitting hens which can result in squashed chicks, put a large marble about three times the size of a normal egg in with their clutch. This will tend to keep the hen up off the chicks.
